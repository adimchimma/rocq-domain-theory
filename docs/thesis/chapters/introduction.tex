\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:intro-motivation}

The operational behavior of a program — what it computes on given inputs — can be studied directly by running it. 
But understanding what a program means, in a sense precise enough to support mathematical proof, requires 
something more: a compositional interpretation of the program's syntax in terms of mathematical objects. 
\emph{Denotational semantics} provides this interpretation, assigning to each program a mathematical entity — 
its denotation — in a way that respects the structure of the language. For simple programs, denotations 
are straightforward. For programs with recursion and recursive types, however, the construction of a suitable 
mathematical universe is itself a deep problem.

Consider the recursive definition: let f be the function satisfying f(x) = 1 + f(x). Read as a program, 
this is a definition by self-reference — f is defined in terms of itself. Read as a mathematical equation, 
it has no solution for any x. The program does not compute any result; it diverges for every input. Yet it is a 
syntactically valid, typeable program, so a complete mathematical theory of programming languages must assign to 
it a precise meaning. The answer \emph{domain theory} gives is elegant: interpret $f$ as the \textit{least fixed point} 
of the operator $\Phi(g)(x) = 1 + g(x)$, computed as the supremum of the sequence $\bot, \Phi(\bot), \Phi^{2}(\bot), ...$
in a mathematical structure -- an $\omega$-complete partial order -- where the order is on the amount of information
conveyed by the program, and $\bot$, being the least element, represents "no information". The operator $\Phi$ is
\emph{Scott-continuous}: it preserves the order structure in a way that guarantees this sequence has a supremum, and that
the supremum is the least fixed point. For our example program, that least fixed point is $\bot$ itself, 
correctly capturing the notion of divergence. This result - that every Scott-continuous function on a \emph{pointed} 
$\omega$-\emph{CPO} has a least fixed point, given explicitly as the supremum of its iteration sequence from $\bot$ --
is the \emph{Kleene fixed-point theorem}, and it is the mathematical engine behind the denotational semantics of 
recursive programs.

Recursive programs, however, are only half of the story. Programming languages also admit recursive \textit{types}:
equations such as $D \cong [D \rightarrow D]$, which ask for a mathematical domain isomorphic to its own \textit{continuous}
function space. This equation is not exotic -- it is exactly what is needed to model the untyped lambda calculus, where
any term may be applied to any other. No set-theoretic model satisfies it
\footnote{For any set $S$ with more than one element, $\left|[S \rightarrow S]\right| = |S|^{|S|} > |S|$ by Cantor's theorem, 
so no set $S$ can satisfy $S \cong [S \to S]$.}, but domain theory does, through a more
sophisticated construction: an inverse limit of an approximating sequence of 
domains connected by structure-preserving maps. Where the Kleene theorem works \textit{inside} a single domain, solving
recursive type equations requires working \textit{across families} of domains. This shift demands that the morphisms 
connecting those families carry their own mathematical structure — specifically, an order — and this is precisely what 
enriched category theory provides. This is the passage from domain theory to enriched domain theory, and it is the central 
organizing principle of this thesis.

The enriched perspective is not merely convenient -- it is logically necessary. The inverse-limit construction requires
\textit{embedding-projection} pairs between domains. Informally, such a pair between domains $D$ and $E$ consists of
an "inclusion" map $e : D \rightarrow E$ and a "retraction" $p : E \rightarrow D$: embedding $D$ inside the larger $E$, 
with $p$ recovering the best approximation in $D$ of any element of $E$. Formally, $e$ and $p$ must satisfy $p \circ e = id_{D}$ 
-- the retraction exactly recovers embedded elements -- and $e \circ p \sqsubseteq id_{E}$ -- the composition in the other 
direction is a deflation, approximating but not exceeding the identity. That second condition is an inequality between 
morphisms, which only makes sense when morphisms themselves carry an order structure. For the inverse-limit to exist, 
moreover, composition must interact coherently with that order: given ascending chains of morphisms 
$f_{0} \sqsubseteq f_{1} \sqsubseteq \cdots$ and $g_{0} \sqsubseteq g_{1} \sqsubseteq \cdots$, their compositions must satisfy
$\sqcup_{n}(f_{n} \circ g_{n}) = (\sqcup_{n} \: f_{n}) \circ (\sqcup_{n} \: g_{n})$. This \textit{joint continuity of composition}
-- whose very statement requires hom-sets to be ordered and composition to preserve those orders -- is precisely the structure of a 
\textit{CPO-enriched category}: a category in which every hom-set is a $\CPO$ and composition is Scott-continuous in each argument. This
is the minimal abstraction in which recursive domain equations can be coherently formulated. We develop this theory formally in 
Chapter 2.

The foundational mechanisation of these ideas is Benton, Kennedy, and Varming's \Rocq{} library \cite{BKV2009}, which formalized
$\omega$-$\CPO$s, the Kleene fixed-point theorem, and gave a complete denotational semantics for PCF$_v$ -- a call-by-value language
with product types, booleans, and general recursion -- including full soundness and computational adequacy. For its stated purpose,
this library was complete. Two subsequent developments, however, create the need for a more expressive foundation. First, richer
type systems -- including those with recursive types modeled by equations of the form $D \cong F(D, D)$ -- require the $\CPO$-enriched 
layer that PCF$_v$ adequacy alone does not demand; the mathematical tools needed simply lay outside the scope of \cite{BKV2009}. Second,
the emergence of quantum programming languages with quantum control flow requires generalizing the order structure of domain theory
in a direction the library was never designed to support. Beyond these theoretical extensions, there is also a practical dimension: 
the Hierarchy Builder framework \cite{HB2023} -- now the standard approach for hierarchical mathematical libraries in Rocq -- postdates 
\cite{BKV2009} entirely, and the hand-assembled infrastructure of that era makes extending the library difficult in practice. 
This thesis takes \cite{BKV2009} as its classical foundation and extends it in both directions.

The choice to develop this theory as a mechanized library reflects the particular demands of the subject. \emph{Proof assistants} reject
informal argument: every definition must be fully specified and every proof must close under \emph{kernel} verification. In a subject 
as technically intricate as domain theory -- where subtle conditions on order, continuity, and chain stability interact throughout
-- this discipline is not merely convenient but epistemically significant. Mechanisation also enables a qualitatively different form 
of reuse: once $\qCPO$ is registered as a \textbf{CPOEnrichedCat} instance, \Rocq{}'s elaborator automatically locates and applies every 
enriched theorem to quantum domain-contexts, with the kernel verifying that all hypotheses are satisfied. Unlike citing a paper theorem, 
this carries machine-checked evidence that every implicit hypothesis is satisfied and no condition has been silently assumed. Finally, 
the Hierarchy Builder framework makes the deep dependency structure of domain theory -- where each algebraic layer extends the last -- 
manageable in a way that hand-assembled infrastructure cannot sustain at scale.

Quantum programs introduce a further and independent motivation for enriched domain theory. Most existing quantum languages --
Proto-Quipper, Quipper, and their relatives -- support only \textit{classical control flow} over quantum data: the decision of which 
branch to take is always a classical computation on classical information. \emph{Quantum control flow} -- branching on unmeasured
quantum states -- is a strictly more expressive paradigm. The canonical example is the \textit{quantum switch} \cite{CDPV2013}: 
given operations $A$ and $B$ and a control qubit in state $\alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle$, apply $A$ then $B$ on the $|0\rangle$
branch and $B$ then $A$ on the $|1\rangle$ branch, without measuring the qubit. The result operates coherently on a superposition of
causal orders, a process with no classical analogue and one that has since been realized experimentally across multiple physical
platforms [placeholder citation]. Classical domain theory cannot model this: its order structure $x \sqsubseteq y$ is Boolean-valued -- 
a proposition that is simply true or false -- and cannot itself carry quantum information or exist in superposition. Modeling 
quantum-coherent ordering requires a richer structure. The natural replacement is a \emph{quantale-valued} order $R(x, y) \in Q$, 
where $Q$ is an \textit{involutive quantale} -- a noncommutative lattice whose elements carry quantum information, defined precisely 
in \cref{sec:disc-qcpo}. This yeilds \emph{quantum CPOs} ($\qCPO$s), introduced by Kornell, Lindenhovius, and Mislove \cite{KLM2024}, who 
show that the resulting category is algebraically compact in the relevant sense: recursive type equations have canonical solutions, 
providing the semantic foundations needed for quantum type systems with recursion. What makes this generalization particularly significant 
for this thesis is a structural observation: given quantum $\CPO$s $X$ and $Y$, the set of quantum Scott continuous maps 
$[X \rightarrow_{q} Y]$ carries a pointwise quantum order that is itself a $\CPO$: $\qCPO$s, in other words, have hom-sets that are 
themselves $\CPO$s -- precisely the definition of a $\CPO$-enriched category. As a result, the same abstract enriched framework developed 
for classical recursive types applies to quantum domains without modification.

This thesis presents a unified, Modern Rocq library formalizing this perspective, taking \cite{BKV2009} as its classical starting point 
and building outward. On the classical side, we modernize and substantially extend the original development: the full order-theoretic
and $\CPO$-theoretic foundations, the Kleen fixed-point theorem with its internalized form \textbf{FIXP} as a scott-continuous operator 
on the function-space $\CPO$, teh lift monad with a fully proved suprememum  construction, and a complete PCF$_v$ adequacy theorem via
a type-index logical relation. The enriched layer formalizes $\CPO$-enriched categories, locally continuous functors, enriched natural
transformations, and the complete framework for mixed-variance recursive domain equations -- mixed-variance locally continuous 
bifunctors, EP-pair lifting, the approximating sequence $D_0, F(D_0, D_0), F^2(D_0, D_0), ...$ with its ep-chain structure, and a 
precise record specification, \textbf{BilimitData}, of what constitutes a bilimit solution. All consequences of a bilimit -- including 
the isomorphism $D_{\infty} \cong F(D_{\infty}, D_{\infty})$ with explicit \textbf{ROLL} and \textbf{UNROLL} morphisms and the 
convergence of the deflation chain to teh identity -- are derived from a single axiom asserting bilimit existence. That axiom's sole 
proof obligation is the construction of an $\omega$-product $\CPO$, a standard result whose formalization we leave to future work. The 
BilimitData record precisely characterizes what that formalization must establish, making the remaining proof obligation explicit. 
On the quantum side, we formalize involutive quantales, quantum posets, and quantum $\CPO$s, and register $\qCPO$ as a 
\textbf{CPOEnrichedCat} instance -- obtaining EP-pairs, locally continuous functors, and the recursive domain equation framework 
for quantum domains as immediate corrollaries of the general enriched theory.
